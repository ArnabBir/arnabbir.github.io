Performance: measure the right thing

metrics

For concurrency, throughput and tail latency usually matter more than single-operation service time. Many slowdowns are caused by:

  • lock contention (serialization)
  • context switching (too many runnable threads)
  • false sharing / cache line ping-pong
  • allocation and GC pressure in hot paths

Scalability Lab: Amdahl’s Law & the Serial Ceiling

interactive

Amdahl’s Law quantifies why “more threads” doesn’t automatically mean “more speed”. Adjust the parallel fraction p and core count n to see diminishing returns and the hard ceiling imposed by serial work.

p includes work that scales with cores.
Speedup = 1 / ((1−p) + (p/n))
tune slidersLook for the “knee” where adding cores stops helping.

Reduce lock contention: a playbook

tuning

1) Reduce lock hold time: move work out of critical sections.

2) Reduce lock granularity: lock splitting/striping; separate independent state.

3) Avoid exclusive locks: use read-write locks or lock-free/atomic techniques where appropriate.

4) Eliminate sharing: thread confinement or sharded ownership (true scalability often comes from not sharing at all).

Micro-benchmark warnings

pitfall

Concurrency performance is often dominated by scheduler effects and JVM warmup. Avoid naive microbenchmarks. Prefer JMH and measure under realistic contention and allocation patterns.
